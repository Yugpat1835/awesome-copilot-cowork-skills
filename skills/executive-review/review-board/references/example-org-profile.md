# Organisation Profile: Meridian Workflow GmbH (worked example)

This is a fully filled example of `org-profile-template.md` for a FICTIONAL company. Meridian Workflow GmbH does not exist; every name and number below is invented for illustration. ## Business model

B2B workflow automation software for mid-market manufacturers, sold as an annual subscription per production site. Three tiers: Standard at 18,000 euros per site per year, Professional at 32,000, Enterprise at 55,000 with on-premise deployment. Subscriptions are 84 per cent of revenue; the remaining 16 per cent is implementation and integration services delivered by an in-house team of 11 consultants. Net revenue retention last year: 104 per cent.

## Customers and segments

214 customers across 460 paying sites, concentrated in Germany, Austria, Switzerland and the Nordics. Core segment: discrete manufacturers with 200 to 2,000 employees (automotive suppliers, industrial equipment, packaging). Largest customer is 6 per cent of revenue. The Professional tier is growing fastest; Standard-tier churn rose to 11 per cent last year, mostly to a cheaper Polish competitor, FlowPilot.

## Stage and size

198 employees, 27.4 million euros annual recurring revenue, growing 19 per cent year on year. Founder-led, majority family-owned, one minority growth-equity investor (since 2023) holding 24 per cent with a board seat. Profitable at a 9 per cent EBITDA margin. No plans to raise or sell in the next two years.

## Current priorities

1. Lift net revenue retention from 104 to 112 per cent by expanding existing accounts to more sites.
2. Ship the AI-assisted scheduling module (codename Taktzeit) to general availability by Q1 next year.
3. Reduce Standard-tier churn below 8 per cent.
4. Open the French market with two reference customers signed by year end.
5. Complete ISO 27001 certification by December (customer security questionnaires now block roughly one deal per month).

## Constraints

Engineering capacity is fixed at 62 people for the next 12 months (hiring pause agreed with the board). All customer data must remain in EU data centres; two large customers have contractual on-premise clauses. New vendor spend above 50,000 euros per year requires board approval. The services team is at 91 per cent utilisation and cannot absorb new implementation load without hiring.

## Recent strategic decisions

Standardised the product on Azure (migration from a Frankfurt co-location finished eight months ago). Killed the consumer-facing product line two years ago after 18 months of losses; the board will not revisit B2C. Declined a distribution partnership with a large ERP vendor last year over data-sharing terms. Switched from perpetual licences to subscriptions in 2022; 31 legacy perpetual customers remain and are sensitive about forced migration.

## Sensitivities and no-go areas

No customer production data may be used to train shared or cross-customer AI models; two flagship customers have this in writing. Burned once by a four-country simultaneous market entry in 2021 that was rolled back after nine months; the board is allergic to multi-market launches. Price increases above 5 per cent per year trigger escalation clauses in the 40 largest contracts. The founder will not approve layoffs as a cost lever; any business case relying on headcount reduction is dead on arrival.
